Data Agent Manifesto: Why Data Agents Matter for the Future of Analysis

Our thesis in one sentence: the scarce resource in analytics was never SQL — it was context, verification, and time, and the agentic loop is the first architecture that returns all three.

The thesis in 60 words

The data agent manifesto argues that analysis should move from static dashboards to agents that plan, query, verify, and explain each answer. Data agents matter because context — not SQL — was the real bottleneck. Agentic loops with evidence trails now make machine-run analysis reviewable, so the analyst's job moves up the stack instead of disappearing.

The bottleneck we normalized

Every data team you have worked on runs a queue. The "quick data pull" waits behind sprint work, the new question waits behind the quick pulls, and by the time the answer lands, the decision was often made without it.

Dashboards were supposed to fix this, and for known questions they did. But a dashboard is a cache of answers to questions someone predicted at build time — ask anything new and you are back in the queue.

The industry has treated this as normal for so long that we budget for the backlog instead of questioning it. Gartner coined "augmented analytics" in 2017, and nearly a decade later the standard fix is still the same: hire more analysts, build more dashboards.

This page is the argument for a different contract between questions and answers. If you want the neutral category definition first, start with what is a data agent — this essay is its opinionated companion.

Five theses

The manifesto stands on five arguments. Each is sourced and stated plainly enough that you can disagree with it precisely.

Thesis 1: SQL was never the bottleneck — context was

On the BIRD benchmark, human data engineers reach 92.96% execution accuracy while models still trail that bar. Look at the failure cases and the pattern is plain: systems stumble on messy schemas and undefined business terms, not on SQL syntax.

A model that does not know your company defines "active user" as 30-day rolling will compute the 7-day version with full confidence. That is a context failure, and model scale does not fix it — retrieval of data dictionaries, metric definitions, and past cases does.

This is why serious systems put retrieval and agent memory at the center of the architecture, not the model. The model is the engine; context is the fuel.

Thesis 2: Dashboards are answers to last year's questions

A dashboard encodes the questions your team knew to ask when it was built. It is a snapshot of past curiosity, refreshed on a schedule.

New questions — the ones that follow a surprise, a competitor move, an anomaly — have no tile. Since 2017 the industry has added assistance to BI under the augmented analytics label, but a human still drives every step, so the queue survives; we unpack that boundary in AI-native vs augmented analytics.

Thesis 3: Analysis is a loop, not a query

Watch a good analyst work: question, plan, query, sanity check, re-plan, query again, explain. The ReAct paper (2022) formalized why this loop beats single-pass generation — interleaving reasoning with actions measurably reduces error.

Anthropic's Building Effective Agents draws the same line: agents are LLMs dynamically directing their own processes and tool usage. Agentic analytics is that loop with data permissions and a human review gate attached.

Thesis 4: Evidence trails are the new trust currency

In the dashboard era you trusted a number because you trusted the person who built the view. In the agent era, trust must attach to the artifact itself: the plan, the queries, the checks, and the sources behind every answer.

Regulators have already moved. The NIST AI Risk Management Framework (2023) organizes AI governance into govern, map, measure, and manage, and the EU AI Act entered into force on 2024-08-01 with obligations phasing in through 2026-2027.

An answer without an evidence trail is not faster analytics — it is unmanaged risk. This is the core case for explainable AI data analysis.

Thesis 5: The analyst's job moves up the stack — it does not disappear

Both 2025 surveys of the field — LLM/Agent-as-Data-Analyst and A Survey of Data Agents — describe the same division of labor: agents execute, humans frame questions, govern definitions, and verify. The repetitive layer of the job is what gets absorbed.

An analyst who reviews ten agent plans a day covers more decisions than one who hand-writes ten queries. We map the emerging skill profile in the AI data analyst job description.

What this means for you

If you lead a data team

Your backlog is the strongest evidence for this manifesto, so measure it: count last quarter's ad-hoc requests and their median wait time. Then pilot an agent on read-only credentials against three of those requests and compare evidence trails, not demo videos.

If you are an analyst

The skills that compound from here are question framing, metric governance, and verification — the parts agents cannot own. Treat the agent as a junior who never sleeps but must always show its work; your sign-off becomes the product.

If you run the business

Stop accepting "the data team is swamped" as a law of nature. Ask one question of any analytics investment: when a brand-new question arrives on Tuesday, what exists by Wednesday?

The counterarguments, taken seriously

A manifesto you cannot argue with is just marketing. Here are the four strongest objections we hear, with what each gets right.

ObjectionWhat it gets rightWhat it misses
"Agents hallucinate"LLMs do fabricate, and a confident wrong number is worse than a slow right oneHallucination is a workflow problem with workflow fixes: plan review, verification checks, read-only credentials, evidence trails
"Governance has not caught up"NIST AI RMF is voluntary, and EU AI Act obligations are still phasing in through 2026-2027The frameworks exist now — NIST AI RMF (2023) and ISO/IEC 42001:2023 — so teams can govern today instead of waiting
"Dashboards still work"For stable, daily-checked metrics they stay cheaper and faster than any agentThe manifesto argues both-and: dashboards for known questions, agents for new ones
"The category is hype"A 2025 survey literally asks "Emerging Paradigm or Overstated Hype?" — skepticism is warrantedThe same survey catalogs working architectures; the answer is scope discipline, not dismissal

The first objection deserves the most respect, and our position is strict: never deploy an agent whose verification steps you cannot inspect. The safeguard patterns are cataloged in our autonomous data agent guide.

On the second, the vocabulary for managing this risk already exists — the NIST AI RMF and ISO/IEC 42001:2023 give your security team a shared structure. Waiting for regulation to settle is itself a risk decision.

On the fourth, we cite the "Overstated Hype?" survey by name because honest manifestos engage their best critics. Where agents are oversold — full autonomy on write-access production data — we side with the skeptics.

Predictions we are willing to be wrong about

A manifesto should make falsifiable claims. These are expectations, not statistics — hold us to them.

  • We expect evidence trails to become a standard line item in analytics procurement reviews as EU AI Act obligations finish phasing in through 2027.
  • We expect plan review — a human gate before execution — to remain the default for high-stakes analysis, not a transitional crutch that autonomy outgrows.
  • We expect evaluation to shift from single-query benchmarks such as Spider and BIRD toward end-to-end task completion with verification.
  • We expect analyst job postings to ask for agent supervision skills — plan review, evidence audit, definition governance — before they drop SQL requirements.
  • We expect the loudest failures in this category to come from agents deployed without read-only credentials and review gates, not from model quality.

FAQ

What is the data agent manifesto?
The data agent manifesto is an argued position: analytics should move from static dashboards and ticket queues to agents that plan, execute, verify, and explain analysis on demand. It is a thesis about where analysis work is heading, not a product specification, and this page lays out the five arguments behind it.
Why do data agents matter?
Data agents matter because most new business questions never fit an existing dashboard, so they wait in an analyst queue. An agent that retrieves business context, plans an analysis, runs it across sources, and shows its evidence turns that wait from days into minutes — while keeping the result reviewable by a human.
Will data agents replace data analysts?
We argue no. Agents absorb the repetitive layer of analysis work: routine pulls, first-pass exploration, and report assembly. Human analysts move up the stack to question framing, metric governance, verification of agent output, and decisions. The 2025 research surveys describe the same division of labor between agent execution and human judgment.
How is this different from BI modernization?
BI modernization upgrades the dashboard stack: faster warehouses, better semantic layers, cleaner visuals. The manifesto argues the unit of work itself changes — from maintaining pre-built views to running governed analysis loops on demand. You still need modeled data; you stop assuming every question must become a dashboard first.
What should teams do first?
Start with three real questions your team answered manually last quarter, including one cross-source question. Pilot an agent on read-only credentials, review its plans before execution, and score the evidence trail against your known answers. Fix metric definitions before scaling: an agent will faithfully automate any ambiguity you leave in place.
What are the main risks of data agents?
The main risks are hallucinated or subtly wrong results, over-broad data access, and audit gaps. Mitigations exist for each: plan review before execution, verification steps with visible checks, read-only scoped credentials, and evidence trails. Governance frameworks such as the NIST AI Risk Management Framework give teams a shared structure for managing all three.
When is a dashboard still the right answer?
A dashboard remains the right tool when the question is stable, the audience is broad, and the metric is checked daily — operational monitoring, executive scorecards, regulated reporting. Agents earn their cost on new, cross-source, or causal questions. The honest architecture runs both, each where it is cheapest.
